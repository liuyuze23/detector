Abstract: CdZnTe is a promising material for the current generation of free electron laser light
sources and future laser-drivenγ-ray sources which require detectors capable of high ﬂux imaging
atX-rayand γ-rayenergies( > 10keV). However,athighﬂuxesCdZnTehasbeenshowntopolarise
due to hole trapping, leading to poor performance. Novel Redlen CdZnTe material with improved
holetransportpropertieshasbeendesignedforhighﬂuxapplications. SmallpixelCdZnTedetectors
were fabricated by Redlen Technologies and ﬂip-chip bonded to PIXIE ASICs. An XIA Digital
Gamma Finder PIXIE-16 system was used to digitise each of the nine analogue signals with a
timing resolution of 10ns. Pulse shape analysis was used to extract the rise times and amplitude
of signals. These were measured as a function of applied bias voltage and used to calculate the
mobility (µ) and mobility-lifetime (µτ) of electrons and holes in the material for three identical
detectors. The measured values of the transport properties of electrons in the high-ﬂux-capable
materialwaslowerthanpreviouslyreportedforRedlenCdZnTematerial( µeτe∼ 1× 10−3cm2V−1
and µe ∼ 1000cm2V−1s−1) while the hole transport properties were found to have improved
(µhτh∼ 3× 10−4cm2V−1 and µh∼ 100cm2V−1s−1).
Keywords: Charge induction; Materials for solid-state detectors; Gamma detectors (scintillators,
CZT, HPG, HgI etc); X-ray detectors

1 Introduction
CdZnTe has become the semiconductor of choice as a direct conversion detection material for high
energyX-raysand γ-rays,duetoawidebandgapof1.44eVat300Kandexcellentelectrontransport
properties,leadingtoalowleakagecurrentandhighspectroscopicresolutionatroomtemperature.
A high eﬀective atomic mass (Zeﬀ∼ 50) and density of 5.8gcm−3 provides good stopping power
forhighenergyphotons,makingCdZnTethecurrentﬁrstchoiceforpixelatedx-rayandgamma-ray
detectors at room temperature [1].
High-ﬂux detection is desirable for use in several applications such as imaging at next gen-
eration free electron light sources and medical imaging, in which detection of a photon ﬂux of
> 108 photons cm−2s−1 is required. The ﬂux that can be achieved with standard CdZnTe is cur-
rentlylimitedto ∼afewMcpsmm −2 [2]bypolarisation,inwhichholestrappedbydefectsgenerate
space charge, resulting in a non-uniform electric ﬁeld [3]. Therefore, one method for improv-
ing detection at high X-ray ﬂuxes requires the development of CdZnTe with better hole transport
properties (e.g. charge carrier mobility and lifetime), in which hole trapping is reduced.
Recently, Redlen Technologies have produced an alternate growth and fabrication process
that allows production of CdZnTe with improved hole transport properties and reduced trapping
optimised for high ﬂux applications. In order to characterise the charge transport properties of
this material, small pixel CdZnTe devices were ﬂip-chip bonded to the PIXIE ASIC, which has

2 Theory
2.1 Shockley-Ramo theorem
The Shockley-Ramo theorem describes the process of charge induction in a detector due to the
motion of charge carriers using the concept of a weighting potential. The weighting potential,
ϕ0(x), is derived by solving the Laplace equation for the conditions where the electrode of interest
is at unity and the remaining electrodes are held at zero potential for no trapped charges [5, 6].
Figure 1. Weighting potentials for small pixels at diﬀerent pitches, with a detector thickness of 2mm.

cathodetoanode. Inadetectorinwhichthepixelsaresmallrelativetothethicknessofthedetector
material,theweightingpotentialbecomesconstrainedclosetothepixel. Weightingpotentialsfora
2mmthickdetectorwithdiﬀerentpixelpitches,showninﬁgure1,werecalculatedusingSentaurus
TCAD, a branch of electronic design software used to model the operation of semiconductor
devices[7]. Theinducedchargeonanelectrodeisproportionaltothechangeinweightingpotential
that a charge experiences as it moves through the detector. If a negative bias voltage is applied to
the large planar electrode then the signal in the detector pixels will be dominated by the transport
of electrons. If the bias voltage is positive then it will be the transport of holes that dominates the
signal. This is known as the small pixel eﬀect [8].

3 Experimental Details
An Application Speciﬁc Integrated Circuit (ASIC), known as the PIXIE ASIC, was developed at
STFC RAL to study charge induction eﬀects in pixelated detectors. [4] The ASIC consists of four
arrays of nine channels, three with pitches of 250µm and one with 500µm. Each pixel contains a
charge sensitive pre-ampliﬁer, output buﬀers and leakage current compensation circuits designed
toprovideimmunitytoleakagecurrentupto250pA.Thepixelsdonotcontainanyshapingcircuits
— instead, the analogue waveform is sampled directly from the chip. The ASIC is also able to
operateintworanges,highgain(formeasurementsupto150keV)andlowgain(formeasurements
up to 1.5MeV). In high gain mode the ASIC is limited to a leakage current of∼ 250pA which, for
the current detectors, limited the ASIC operation to the low gain mode [4].
Whilst only one electrode on the CdZnTe detector was pixelated, by switching the polarity of
the applied bias, both holes and electrons were detected. While the PIXIE ASIC can measure both
positiveandnegativepolaritysignals, theleakagecurrentcompensationcircuithasbeenoptimised
for the detection of electrons. This limited the measurement of hole signals to smaller biases than
were used to study electrons.

4 Pulse processing techniques
The PIXIE-16 system recorded the digitised pulse shape data in 16-bit binary data ﬁles. Data
was recorded with detection thresholds of 22.0ADU (Analogue-to-Digital Units) (∼ 2.8keV) and
40.0ADU(∼ 5.6keV)forelectronsandholesrespectively;thesewereimplementedinthePIXIE-16
control software and were used to distinguish real events from low energy noise introduced by the
electronics and cross-talk between pixels.
Charge sharing (CS) in which the charge generated from one X-ray interaction is collected
on multiple pixels due to charge carrier diﬀusion is undesirable as it incorrectly introduces low
energyeventsinmultiplepixels. InordertoaccuratelyaccountforCS,onlyeventswherethepulse
amplitude in the central pixel exceeds the set threshold were recorded. As the central pixel is fully
surrounded by neighbouring pixels, CS events were accurately identiﬁed from use of coincidence
timing. Any frames of data with two or more pixels over the set threshold were not included in the
data analysis.
BespokecodewaswritteninMatlabtoextractkeycharacteristicsofeachoftherecordedpulse
shapeswhichareextractedfromthebinaryoutputofthedigitiser. Onceextracted,pulseshapeswere
smoothed with a ﬁrst order Savitsky-Golay ﬁlter (SGF) using a frame length of 15 [13] as shown
in ﬁgure 3a. This ﬁltering reduced the RMS noise by 4ADU (0.56keV). An interpolation of 10
pointsbetweeneachdatapointwasalsoimplementedtoimprovethecalculationofpulserisetimes.
The amplitude of each smoothed pulse was extracted and binned into pulse height spectra
with a bin size of 0.5keV. Characteristic peak positions at 59.54keV (Am-241γ-ray), 17.72keV
(Lβ Neptunium peak) and 13.95keV (Lα Neptunium peak) were identiﬁed from the electron pulse
height spectra and centroids calculated using a Gaussian ﬁt to the spectral peaks. A linear ﬁt was
usedtoconvertfromunitsofADUtokeVasshowninﬁgure3b. Thisgaveanenergycalibrationof
∼ 0.14keV per ADU. Values of the FWHM of the 59.54keV peak were∼ 1.8keV for both pitches
at−1000V, as previously reported [4].

6 Conclusion and discussion
Table 1 summarises the results of the charge transport characterisation of three high-ﬂux CdZnTe
crystals using these methods. It is clear that the electron charge carrier transport properties in the
high-ﬂux material are approximately an order of magnitude poorer than the standard spectroscopic
grade Redlen CdZnTe. In comparison, the hole lifetime is over an order of magnitude greater than
that previously reported and is now very similar to that of the electrons.
This improvement in the hole lifetime was likely due to a reduction in the concentration of

desirable, standard Redlen CdZnTe is the most suitable material due to the better electron charge
