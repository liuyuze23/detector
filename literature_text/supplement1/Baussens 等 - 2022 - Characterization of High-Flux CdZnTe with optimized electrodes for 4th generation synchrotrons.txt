Abstract: In this collaboration work between IMEM-CNR and the ESRF, we explore the poten-
tial of the Redlen Technologies High-Flux Cadmium Zinc Telluride (HF-CZT) material for direct
X-ray detection at ﬂuxes ranging from 10+7 to 10+12 photons mm−2 s−1. HF-CZT single crystals
with optimized electroless gold and sputtered platinum electrodes are characterized both under
dark conditions and under X-ray irradiation. We report low leakage current under dark conditions
(<100 pA/mm2 at 5 kV/cm). We also report good linearity (R2> 0.999) under moderate to high
incident X-ray ﬂux (10+7 to 10+10 photons mm−2 s−1) as well as good stability and reproducibility
for incident X-ray ﬂuxes up to10+12 photons mm−2 s−1. Finally, we study transient phenomena in
the seconds time range and report fast stabilization time (<200 ms) and negligible afterglow in this
time range.
Keywords: Instrumentation for synchrotron radiation accelerators; X-ray detectors

1 Introduction
With the rise of 4th Generation Synchrotron Light Sources such as the Extremely Brilliant Source
(EBS) of the ESRF [1], the need for direct X-ray detection under high photon ﬂux (>10+7 photons
mm−2 s−1) with moderate to high photon energy (30–100 keV range) has increased. Semiconductor
materials with high atomic number that can be grown into thick ( ⩾1 mm) single crystals are
needed for high energy detection. In that respect, Cadmium Zinc Telluride (CdZnTe or CZT)
is a good candidate. The CZT single crystals grown with the Traveler Heat Method (THM)
can be thicker than 10 mm and have demonstrated high electron transport properties (μτe> 10−2
cm2/V) [2]. However, their poorer hole transport properties have been an issue at high ﬂux as they
lead to radiation-induced polarization phenomena. Recently, Redlen Technologies succeeded in
overcoming the issue by enhancing the hole transport properties (μτh> 10−4 cm2/V) for high-ﬂux
applications [2]. This novel high-ﬂux CZT (HF-CZT) seems promising as it limits the polarizing
phenomena observed in standard CZT at high photon ﬂux [ 2, 3]. However, when it comes to
electrical contacts, it appears that the standard electroless gold blocking contacts lead to much
higher leakage current in HF-CZT than in standard CZT. The IMEM-CNR laboratory (Parma) has
been developing novel contacts more suited to the HF-CZT. In this work, Redlen Technologies HF-
CZT single crystals with IMEM-CNR optimized electrodes are characterized at the ESRF under
both dark conditions and high-ﬂux (>10+7 photons mm−2 s−1) X-ray irradiation. It is the ﬁrst time,
to the best of our knowledge, that the Redlen Technologies HF-CZT has been characterized under
X-ray ﬂuxes above10+6 photons mm−2 s−1 [4].

2 Methods: HF-CZT detectors
HF-CZT detectors were purchased from Redlen Technologies and re-processed in IMEM-CNR.
Two samples, with approximate dimensions 5× 5 mm2 and thickness 1.5 mm, were fabricated
with identical electrode layout: planar cathode and pixelated anode (ﬁgure1(a)). The anode layout
consists of two single pixels and a2× 2 pixel matrix. All of the pixels are 500μms q u a r e s .O nt h e
anode side, the two samples have sputtered platinum electrodes. On the cathode side, sample 1 has
an electroless gold electrode while sample 2 has a sputtered platinum electrode. The main char-
acteristics of the two samples are summarized in table1. Both samples were mounted onto PCBs
using bicomponent epoxy glue. They were then bounded using copper wires previously soldered
onto the PCBs and glued on the pixels with conductive silver epoxy (ﬁgure 1(b)). During mea-
surements, the samples were placed in a custom made aluminium alloy shielding box (ﬁgure1(c)).
The shielding box provided dark conditions, its entrance window was in opaque kapton tape. The
temperature inside the shielding box was controlled using a Julabo FL300 recirculating cooler and
measured with a Pt100 probe. To prevent water condensation, a nitrogen ﬂow was maintained
inside the shielding box. All characterizations discussed in this paper were performed on single
pixel 1 (pixel on the top left hand corner in ﬁgure1(a)). The cathode of the sample under test was
negatively biased using a ISEG SHQ 224 high voltage power supply and its current was measured
using a Keithley 6485 picoammeter.
Figure 1. (a) On the left, the planar cathode. On the right, the pixelated anode layout. (b) Sample 2 mounted
onto its PCB. (c) Shielding box.
In the interest of improved readability, and because the results of sample 1 and 2 were equivalent
for all the characterizations carried out, only the results of sample 2 will be presented throughout
the paper. The results of sample 1 are available in the supplementary material and will be discussed
alongside the results of sample 2 in the relevant sections of this paper.

3 Dark conditions measurements
The I-V characteristics of both samples were measured at20 ◦C under dark conditions between 0
and −1000 V using−10 V steps. Figure 2 displays the I-V characteristic of sample 2. Figure S1
(supplementary material) displays the I-V characteristic of sample 1. For both samples, an
unexpected peak of current is visible at low bias on the forward scan of the I-V characteristics.
This peak is believed to be related to the inaccuracy of the instrumentation at low voltage. It
can also be noted that the I-V characteristics of both samples present some hysteresis but this
phenomenon is very limited with the deviation between the forward and reverse scans being less
than 10%. The dark current densities at −1000 V (−5 kV/cm) of sample 1 and 2 (Au/CZT/Pt
and Pt/CZT/Pt) are 90 pA/mm2 a n d6 0p A / m m2 respectively. These dark current density values
constitute a 1 order of magnitude improvement compared with the standard Redlen Technologies
pixelated HF-CZT (2 mm thick) [3] and a 4 orders of magnitude improvement compared with other
electrode conﬁgurations (Au/CZT/Au and Pt/CZT/Au) previously tested on HF-CZT at IMEM-
CNR. These comparisons underline the role of the platinum anode in the leakage current reduction.
To conclude, the dark current results conﬁrm that the electrodes optimized by IMEM-CNR are
successful blocking contacts.

4 Behaviour under X-ray irradiation
4.1 Moderate to high X-ray ﬂux at the BM05 beamline
Both samples were characterized under moderate to high X-ray ﬂux ( 3× 10+7 to 8× 10+9
photons mm−2 s−1) at the BM05 beamline of the ESRF using a 20 keV monochromatic syn-
chrotron beam. The beam was collimated to the pixel size (500 μm × 500 μm). The incident ﬂux
was adjusted using aluminium attenuators and measured with a 500μm silicon diode.
Three measurements were performed on each samples using the setup described: ﬁrst, a pulsed
irradiation measurement, then, anI-V characteristic measurement, and ﬁnally, a long term stability
measurement at a given bias and ﬂux. The objective was to verify the stability and the linearity of
the samples with the incident X-ray ﬂux.
The pulsed irradiation measurement consisted in submitting the sample under test, biased at
−1000 V, to increasing X-ray ﬂuxes. Seven X-ray ﬂuxes within the ﬂux range were tested. Each
ﬂux was applied for 10 minutes. Between ﬂux steps, the beam was cut oﬀ for 5 minutes using the
maximum thickness of Al attenuators available (11.5 mm). The current of the sample under test
was recorded every 200 ms for the entire duration of the test. Figure3 shows the pulsed irradiation
characterization of sample 2 as well as its linearity curve with incident ﬂux. The results of sample
1 are available in ﬁgure S2 of the supplementary material. Both samples show good stability and
good linearity for all the ﬂuxes tested.
Figure 3. On the left, 20 keV pulsed irradiation of sample 2 measured on the BM05 beamline. In red, above
each pulse, the corresponding incident ﬂux in photons per millimeter squared per second. On the right, the
linearity curve of sample 2 with incident X-ray ﬂux.
For the I-V characteristics and the time stability measurements three incident ﬂuxes were

The characterizations performed on the BM05 beamline of the ESRF demonstrated that
the samples are stable and linear under incident X-ray ﬂuxes ranging from 3× 10+7 to 8× 10+9
photons mm−2 s−1. However, ongoing detector developments of the ESRF require sensors capable
of sustaining ﬂuxes up to 10+10 photons mm−2 s−1. This ﬂux cannot be reached with the current
setup available at the BM05 beamline. For this reason, another set of characterizations was carried
out at the ID19 beamline of the ESRF.
4.2 High to very high X-ray ﬂux at the ID19 beamline
The ID19 beamline of the ESRF is capable of reaching ﬂuxes several orders of magnitude
beyond10+10 photons mm−2 s−1. However, we could only access the beamline for a very limited
amount of time and thus only platinum electrodes (sample 2) could be tested. Two characteriza-
tions were performed: pulsed irradiation measurements andI-V characteristic measurements. Both
characterizations were performed using a 19 keV 1% monochromatic synchrotron beam produced
by the U32 insertion device of the beamline [5].
The pulsed irradiation measurements were performed for four ﬂuxes ranging between2× 10+9
and 1× 10+12 photons mm−2 s−1. The incident ﬂux was adjusted by varying the gap of the undulator.
For each of the four ﬂuxes, sample 2 was irradiated during 10 s. Between ﬂux steps, the beam was
cut oﬀ for 15 s. The pulsed irradiation measurement was repeated three times and was found to
be reproducible. Figure 5 shows one pulsed irradiation characterization of sample 2. The current
density of sample 2 is stable for all the incidents ﬂuxes tested.

current, hypothesizing complete charge collection above−500 V. They were also estimated using

4.3 Afterglow: preliminary results
Afterglow (or residual photocurrent) measurements were not speciﬁcally performed on the samples.
However, it is possible to make observations about afterglow from the pulsed irradiation results
of ﬁgure 3. Because the frequency used to measure the current during the pulsed irradiation
characterization was 5 Hz, the analysis of the afterglow can only be done for time constants above
200 ms. Lower time constants will be investigated in future works.
From ﬁgure 3, three observations can be made regardless of the incident X-ray ﬂux. Firstly,
the rise time for all pulses is below or equivalent to the measurement limit of 200 ms. Secondly,
the photocurrent is stable throughout irradiation, no transient phenomena that could be consistent
with signiﬁcant trapping mechanisms are observed. And thirdly, after the irradiation is stopped, it

5C o n c l u s i o n
The aim of this work was to characterize the high-ﬂux CZT material from Redlen Technologies
under high X-ray ﬂuxes ranging from10+7 to 10+12 photons mm−2 s−1. Two devices with optimized
electrode conﬁgurations (Au/CZT/Pt and Pt/CZT/Pt) were fabricated in IMEM-CNR from the
HF-CZT material. Both of these optimized electrode conﬁgurations demonstrated signiﬁcantly
reduced leakage current (<100 pA/mm2 at−5 kV/cm and at 20◦C) compared with standard Redlen
Technologies pixelated platinum electrode conﬁguration. The linearity, stability and repeatability
of HF-CZT sensors under incident X-ray ﬂux was demonstrated for both electrode conﬁgurations
between
10+7 to 10+10 photons mm−2 s−1. The Pt/CZT/Pt conﬁguration was also measured under
even higher incident X-ray ﬂuxes. From these measurements, the stability and reproducibility of
HF-CZT were demonstrated for X-ray ﬂuxes up to 10+12 photons mm−2 s−1 though a hysteresis
eﬀect was observed on theI-V characteristics above10+11 photons mm−2 s−1. Finally, the afterglow
and stabilization time of HF-CZT in the seconds time range were studied for both electrode
conﬁgurations. In this time range, the afterglow was shown to be negligible and the stabilization
time was shown to be below 200 ms. To conclude, the potential of Redlen Technologies HF-CZT
material for the detection of high-ﬂux X-ray was demonstrated. Moreover, both conﬁgurations,
with Au or Pt cathodes, were shown to be stable and successful blocking contacts, leaving the
choice of a particular conﬁguration to purely technological considerations (deposition process
availability, cost, time constraints. . . ). Future work will be dedicated to the study of the hysteresis
eﬀect observed at very high X-ray incident ﬂux as well as to a more in depth study of the afterglow.
