Abstract:The use of high-Z sensors for scientific applications at synchrotrons will gain more
importance due to the upgrade of many light sources around the world to diffraction limited light
sources for which the photon flux at high photon energies (above 30keV) will significantly increase.
Especially when illuminated with high photon fluxes, many established high-Z sensor materials like
GaAs or Cd(Zn)Te are known to suffer from charge (de-)trapping effects in the bulk or at the contact
interface which will ultimately lead to polarization and afterglow effects in the sensor. Commercially
available high-Z sensors (Chromium-compensated GaAs, CdTe and CdZnTe) have been investigated
at the Material Science beamline of the Swiss Light Source regarding their dynamic behavior when
exposed to photon fluxes between106ph/mm2·s and≈ 5 · 1010ph/mm2·s. In this work, the signal
stability during irradiation will be presented, and a follow-up publication will discuss the afterglow.
Keywords:Hybrid detectors; X-ray detectors

1 Introduction
The most common sensor material for X-ray detectors at light sources is silicon due to its outstanding
crystalline quality and charge transport properties. The biggest drawback for using silicon for higher
photon energies is that its stopping power drops dramatically for photon energies above 15keV due
to its low atomic number of𝑍𝑆𝑖 = 14.
High-Zsensors,suchaschromiumcompensatedGaAs( 𝑍Ga/As = 31/33)orCd(Zn)Te( 𝑍Cd/(Zn)/Te =
48/(30)/52), have much higher atomic numbers and therefore provide reasonably high stopping
powers (> 50%) for photon energies above 50keV for typical sensor thicknesses [1]. However, high-Z
sensors are compound semiconductors and suffer from other short-comings compared to silicon like a
reduced uniformity, worse charge transport properties and especially charge (de-)trapping leading to
afterglow and polarization effects [2–5]. Moreover, heavy materials produce relatively long ranged
auto-fluorescence photons which can affect both the energy and spatial resolution of the detector.
Currently, many light sources around the world are being upgraded to diffraction limited light
sources, increasing the monochromatic coherent photon flux for photon energies above 10keV
significantly [6]. This motivates the development of high-Z sensors for detectors at light sources
to increase the absorption efficiency for photon energies above 15keV. However, the signal quality
may be reduced due to afterglow and polarization phenomena these sensors display which get more
pronounced when they are exposed to high photon intensities [7–9].
The aim of the work described in this paper was to evaluate the dynamic behavior of various
high-Z sensor materials to identify the most suitable alternative to silicon sensors for photon energies
above 15keV at synchrotron sources and FELs. The research goal was to investigate the dynamic
behavior of the high-Z sensors which was separated into two phases: 1) the signal stability during the

2 Experimental setup
2.1 Sensors
Table 1 summarizes the sensors that have been investigated. Different variations of the high-Z
sensors were used which will be explained in the following subsections. The employed silicon
sensors were standard p-in-n sensors.
2.1.1 GaAs:Cr
Chromium compensated GaAs sensors were delivered by the Tomsk State University (TSU) in
Russia. In order to obtain high-resistivity material, commercially available, low resistivity n-type
GaAs wafer are annealed with chromium which diffuses into the GaAs wafer [10]. GaAs:Cr sensors
work as photo resistors, meaning the dark current through the bulk is typically significantly higher
compared to photo diodes. Extensive characterization results with GaAs:Cr sensors have previously
been published [11, 12]. One of the main findings was the short lifetime of holes in the order of
magnitude of a few ns.
For this study, different types of sensors have been tested, which include variations of the
production batch as well as the underlying growth technique of the commercially available n-type
GaAs wafer. Sensors from the production batches from 2017, 2018 and 2019 were produced from
LEC-grown (LiquidEncapsulated Czochralski) GaAs wafers. VGF-grown (Vertical Gradient Freeze)
GaAs wafers were used for the 2021 production batch. The thickness of the sensors was 500μm
and the typical sensor bias voltage is−300V.
2.1.2 CdTe
The CdTe sensors investigated in this study came from Acrorad [13]. Acrorad offers two contact
configurations, which allow the operation of the sensors either as photo resistor (quasi-ohmic type)
or as photo diode (Schottky type). In case of the Schottky type CdTe sensors, the Schottky contact,
consisting of Al, was on the pixel side in order to be able to collect electrons. The backside contact
was made of Pt. Two batches of Schottky sensors were investigated from 2017 and 2019. The
quasi-ohmic type CdTe sensors have Pt contacts on both sides. All CdTe-based sensors are 750μm
thick and were operated with−500V.
2.1.3 CdZnTe
The CdZnTe sensors were obtained from Redlen Technologies [14]. Two different kinds of sensors
are available, called ‘spectroscopic type’ and ‘high flux type’, the latter is optimized to handle higher
photon fluxes compared to the ‘spectroscopic type’ by better matching the𝜇 × τ products of electrons
and holes [15, 16]. The thickness was 1500μm and the operating sensor high voltage−500V.

2.2 Readout chip
Thereadoutchip, thatwasusedfortheexperiments, wastheJUNGFRAUASIC.JUNGFRAUconsists
of 256 × 256 pixels with a pixel pitch of75 × 75 μm2 [17]. It was designed having in mind the high
intensities exhibited at free electron lasers, thus being able to automatically adjust the gain of the
preamplifier to the number of incoming photons on a pixel level by means of three different gain
ranges. This mode, called ‘dynamic gain switching’ mode, was initially not foreseen for electron
collection, and hence high-Z sensors, such that dynamic gain switching in the earlier generation of
JUNGFRAU only works for hole collection. At the time of writing this article, two different iterations
of JUNGFRAU existed, JF1.0 and JF1.1, with the most relevant difference being the ability of JF1.1
to automatically adjust the gain also in case of electron collection. For the experiments presented
in this article, both iterations of the readout chips were used.
Alternatively,itisalsopossibletogloballypredefineandfixthegainrangebeforeeachmeasurement
which was the most common operating mode used in this study. In contrast to dynamic gain switching
mode, where the CDS buffer (=Correlated Double Sampling) is only used for G0, the CDS buffer
is always active in fixed gain mode having the advantage of an improved noise performance of the
gain ranges G1 and G2 at the cost of a reduced dynamic range.
In order to be able to simultaneously use hole-collecting sensors (like Silicon) and electron-
collecting sensors (like high-Z sensors), the working point has been chosen to be in the middle of
the dynamic range, thereby sacrificing the maximum dynamic range. For the fixed gain mode, the
values for the maximum linear dynamic range and noise performance measured with a GaAs:Cr
sensor are given in table 2.
When using 20 keV photons and the typical integration time of 5μs used for this study,
the maximum linear dynamic range in fixed G2 of 25000keV corresponds to a photon flux of
4.4 ·1010 × 20keV photons per mm2 and second. If the pixel is operated in dynamic gain switching
mode and the working point is optimized to collect only one kind of charge carrier, this maximum
linear dynamic range will increase by roughly a factor of 4. (However, it is possible to measure higher
photon fluxes, thereby sacrificing linearity of the readout electronics.)

The analog signal output of the JUNGFRAU readout chip is digitized by means of an analog-
digital-converter (ADC), providing a digital value in the unit of an analog-digital-unit (ADU). The raw
data in units of ADU coming from the detector was corrected in the following order:
•Pedestal correction:as JUNGFRAU is a charge-integrating detector, the raw dark signal is
mainly defined by the working points of the various buffers and by the dark current being
collected from the sensor which strongly depends on the sensor material. The dark signal is
called pedestal and ideally a constant value. The pedestal is measured by acquiring (at least)
1000 dark frames. For each pixel, the values of the dark frames are put into a histogram and
the resulting distribution fitted by a Gaussian function. The mean value of the fit function
corresponds to the pedestal value for the respective pixel. The r.m.s. from the Gaussian fit is the
noise value of the pixel. To perform a pedestal correction, the pedestal value is subtracted from
the measured pixel values on a frame-by-frame basis.
A pixel was considered malfunctioning, if its pedestal value was out of a pre-defined range or if
its noise value was above or below an acceptance window of±10×the r.m.s. of the global noise
distribution. Malfunctioning pixels were excluded from the data analysis.
•Common mode correction:common mode is a form of noise that is causing a temporal change
of the pedestal value affecting several pixels at once and which is often correctable by tracking
the pedestal. In JF1.0, the most occurring common mode contribution is a global shift of
the pedestal value due to a readout buffer which was improved in the subsequent versions of
the readout chip. For each assembly under test, the global pedestal shift of each frame was
determined by measuring the difference of the five outermost pixel rows and columns from the
previously obtained average pedestal value. The obtained correction value was then subtracted
from the pedestal corrected image for all pixels.
•Gain correction:the output of each frame is given in units of ADU. In order to convert the
outputintokeVandtofurthermoretakeintoaccountvariationsofthegainofthedifferentpixels,
the gain of each pixel is measured by illuminating the sensor with monochromatic X-rays. The
photo peak position of the pedestal corrected monochromatic spectra (in ADU) was fitted for
each pixel. By knowing the photon energies, a pixelwise conversion map from ADU into keV,
called gain map, can be calculated. The pedestal corrected values of a pixel in ADU, giving the
net collected charge of each pixel without contributions from dark/leakage current, are divided
by the gain value to obtain the measured photo signal in units of keV.

A MYTHEN strip detector was used to independently monitor the intensity of the incoming
photon flux to be able to separate effects of varying incoming beam intensity from fluctuations of
the measured signal in the sensor e.g., due to polarization effects. The MYTHEN strip detector with
silicon sensor was placed out of the direct beam to measure air-scattered photons. MYTHEN is a
single photon counting strip detector. The counting threshold was set to 50% of the incoming photon
energy of 20keV and the number of photons was continuously counted in acquisition windows of
10 seconds. As the MYTHEN detector sits outside of the beam, the number of counts obtained by
the MYTHEN detector do not represent the absolute photon flux. However, the photon counts from
MYTHEN provide two important pieces of information: first, the photon counts from MYTHEN were
used to monitor the signal stability of the beam during the measurement (with a 10 second precision).
Secondly, it provides a relative measurement of the eight different illumination settings by calculating
the ratios of the photon counts between the different intensities. These ratios were then taken to obtain
the absolute photon fluxes by using the two lowest intensities measured by the JUNGFRAU module in
the direct beam as absolute intensity measurement. The two lowest intensities were chosen such that
the JUNGFRAU detector under test could be operated in high gain (G0), thus being able to provide
precise values of the incoming photon flux. Additionally, the lowest photon fluxes ranged in the order
of 106–107ph/mm2·s, where no polarization effects were expected. If a silicon sensor was available
in the hybrid module, the information of the silicon sensor (in direct beam) was used for the flux
determination, considering the reduced absorption efficiency of the silicon sensor.
3 Results
As explained in previous sections, for each sensor under test, eight different signal transients (with
photon fluxes between∼ 5 × 106ph per mm2·s and∼ 5 × 1010ph per mm2·s) have been recorded.
Typically, the integration time of the JUNGFRAU readout chip was set to 5.0μs. However, in case
of sensors with high dark current like GaAs:Cr or Ohmic CdTe the integration time was reduced
at +30◦C to 2.0μs to retain enough dynamic range. It is worth noting that the integration times
are relatively long compared to the charge collection times which are in the order of few tens of ns
and the preamplifier settling time of 500ns. Therefore, in case of a partial shielding of the electric
field in the sensor due to polarization, which would yield in prolongated charge collection times,
most of the charge carriers would still be collected. In case of a single photon counting detector, the
number of counts would decrease as the signal induction of the charge carriers would be extended
in time, causing longer pulses with a reduced signal height.
The signal stability of the measured signal can be plotted as a function of time after opening
the beam shutter. The average measured signal in keV per pixel was converted into the respective
photon flux per mm2 and second (ph/(mm2·s)) for each exposure. As the detector was operated at a
rate of 200Hz, the temporal resolution was 5ms. The conversion allows to compare the measured
photon flux with the incoming photon flux (which is measured independently by a photon counting
strip detector in scatter mode, compare section 3.4).
For each assembly under test, the dependence of measured signal as function of the incoming
photon flux was plotted and fitted with a linear for times of 10ms, 100ms, 1s, 10s, 100s and 1000s

3.1 High-flux type CdZnTe sensors
High-flux type CdZnTe sensors from Redlen are optimized for high photon flux applications by better
matching the charge transport properties of electrons and holes compared to the more traditional
spectroscopic type CdZnTe sensors [15, 16]. Compared to spectroscopic CdZnTe, where the ratio
between the electron and the hole𝜇 × τ product is roughly 500, this ratio is reduced to 3.8 for
high-flux type CdZnTe, which is mainly achieved by reducing the electron lifetime in favor of the
hole lifetime which is increased.
High-flux type CdZnTe performs close to the ideal behavior: throughout the irradiation time
of 20 minutes, the measured signals remain stable (figure 3) for incoming photon fluxes between
6.7 × 106ph/(mm2·s) and 3.3 × 1010ph/(mm2·s).

3.3 Chromium-compensated GaAs
Another promising, albeit with lower stopping power, high-Z sensor material for high-flux application
is chromium-compensated GaAs, which has been intensively studied by the PSD Detector Group
in the previous years [11, 12, 22].

Compared to CdZnTe, the charge transport properties of GaAs:Cr are inferior, especially the low
hole 𝜇 × τ product which is two to three orders of magnitude less and in the order of< 10−6cm2/V,
causing negative signals in the vicinity of the collecting electrode due to incomplete positive charge
collection. Another obvious disadvantage is the reduced stopping power of GaAs:Cr compared to
Cd(Zn)Te due to its lower atomic number.
Currently, the biggest advantage of using GaAs:Cr is the commercial availability of 4-inch
wafers allowing to produce full-scale sensors with a dimension of8 × 4cm2. Being the standard
size of modules for all detectors produced by the PSD Detector Group, they can be easily integrated
using existing mechanics. If the sensor quality permits, they could be used in areas with a high
photon flux at moderately high photon energies (12–40keV) instead of silicon sensors to prevent
radiation damage to the readout chips.

3.5 CdTe (Quasi-ohmic type)
The transients of quasi-ohmic CdTe sensors exhibit a characteristic increase of the measured signal
which starts to set in after few seconds of stable operation while continuously irradiated (figure 10).
During irradiation, the signal never breaks down.
The fit parameters are closer to the ideal values (𝑚 = 1, 𝑐 = 0) at the beginning of the irradiation,
as especially the strong increase of measured signal at low intensities causes a significant deviation
of the ideal linear response of the sensor. Moreover, a strong temperature dependence can be seen,
it is unclear whether the fit parameters would converge to the same value after an extended period
of time or whether a refresh of the sensor bias would have reverted the sensor performance to its
behavior at the beginning of the irradiation.
4 Summary and conclusions
In conclusion, in none of the investigated regions of the various high-Z sensors the charge collection
wasseverelyhamperedcausingasignificantbreak-downofthemeasuredsignalasitwouldbeexpected
in case of a sensor polarization [8, 9].

However, CdTe sensors of either kind, electron collecting Schottky type and quasi-ohmic type,
suffer from disturbed charge collection during phases of continuous illumination creating instable
signal transients. Potentially, CdTe based sensors might be used in applications with short photon
pulses, but this would require further tests at pulsed sources.
Both, chromium compensated GaAs and CdZnTe (high-flux type and spectroscopic-type),
performed well during the high-flux illumination tests, while the latter performed better for photon
fluxes above3.0 × 1010ph/(mm2·s) (see table 3). It is noteworthy that the signal loss for photon fluxes
around 5 × 1010ph/(mm2·s) is already close to the limit of the linear dynamic range and therefore
part of the observed signal loss is due to a beginning saturation of the readout buffers.
The performance of the CdZnTe sensors regarding signal stability was very close to silicon
sensors which are still considered the state of the art regarding their dynamic behavior. Remarkably,
the 320–450μm thick silicon sensors which were used for reference measurements suffered severely
from the induced radiation damage during the irradiation tests, while the CdZnTe (but also GaAs:Cr)
sensors performance was unaltered.
